Frame Your World!

Clear Vision, Stylish Frames—See the World Differently!

What are eyeglasses?

Eyeglasses are wearable lenses mounted in a frame you wear on your face so you can see better. They do that by correcting refractive errors (like nearsightedness) or other issues that make it hard for you to see properly. Corrective lenses in prescription and reading eyeglasses get their name from how they “correct” your eyesight. They compensate for what your eyes can’t do on their own so you can see clearly.

Eyeglasses (often known simply as “glasses” or “spectacles”) can help with a variety of vision issues. That includes difficulty seeing objects up close, far away or at varying distances. They can also help with distortions in your vision, age-related vision difficulties and eye alignment issues.

What do eyeglasses do?

Eyeglasses do for your eyes what a stepstool does for how high up you can reach. They help you use an ability that isn’t strong enough on its own.

When beams of light enter your eyes, they pass through the cornea and lens. The cornea and lens are supposed to bend light beams so they converge on the retina at just the right distance. When they do this correctly, your vision is clear and sharp.

But if you have a refractive error (either in one or both eyes), the affected eye(s) can’t bend beams of light correctly, making things look blurry and out of focus. That’s where eyeglasses come in. They add more bend to the light than your eyes can do on their own.

What can eyeglasses help with?

  • Nearsightedness (myopia): Objects close up are clear, but objects farther away are blurry.
  • Farsightedness (hyperopia): Objects far away are clear, but objects close up are blurry.
  • Astigmatism: Your vision is blurry close up and far away. It can also cause visible “starburst” rays coming off lights (especially at night).
  • Presbyopia: Trouble focusing up close due to age.

How common are eyeglasses?

Eyeglasses are extremely common. Over 1 billion people around the world wear them.

What tests will I need before getting eyeglasses?

Before you get prescription glasses, you’ll need to undergo an eye exam. An eye care specialist, usually an optometrist or ophthalmologist, will do the exam and associated tests to check your sight. They’ll check if you have 20/20 vision and determine what it’ll take to get you up to that.

How Are Glasses Made?

If you wear glasses, you know what a big difference they make to your quality of life. Your first thought every morning may well be, “Where are my glasses?” such is their importance.

They’re readily available, they improve the lives of everyone who uses them, and if you break or lose them, you know how to go about getting another pair.

Despite being aware of all this, and grateful for their existence, it’s unlikely that you know all of the ins and outs of how they are produced.

In this article, we’ll delve into the intricate process of manufacturing glasses, from frame fabrication to lens crafting, explaining how those specs sitting on the bridge of your nose ended up there.

Creating the Frames

Just as there is a wide variety of eyeglass frame shapes available, there are a number of different frame materials, from metal and plastic to eco-friendly options. Here, we’ll take a look at the processes involved in making acetate frames.

Acetate is a plant-based plastic known for its flexibility and hypoallergenic properties, making it a prime material for eyeglass frames. The journey of turning acetate into fashionable frames involves a few crucial steps.

  • Cutting, counter-boring and riveting: High-tech cutting machinery carves out the desired frame shape. Counter-boring creates recesses for hinges, and riveting secures the hinges in place.
  • Curving, polishing and blasting: Frames are heated, molded, and polished to achieve a smooth, shiny finish. Blasting creates unique matte finishes or further smooths the frame.
  • Temple machining and pad printing: Temples are crafted and refined for comfort, and brands often add their logos using pad printing.

Manufacturing the Lenses

As important as the frames are, the eyeglass lenses are the most essential part of prescription glasses. The production process is almost entirely automated, using cutting-edge technology and machinery.

  • Preparation: The prescription dictates the lens power, curvature, and type. The appropriate material is chosen based on the prescription.
  • Blocking: A protective block is attached to the lens blank to secure it during carving.
  • Generating: Computerized machines grind the lens blank to the correct power and curvature.
  • Polishing and engraving: Lenses are polished for clarity and may be engraved with tiny markings for alignment.
  • Tinting and coating: Lenses may be tinted or coated with anti-reflective, UV-blocking, or scratch-resistant layers.

Lens Fitting

Now that the two main components are ready, it’s time to combine them for the finished product. The frame is gently heated, and the eyeglass lenses are inserted into the groove. This step is commonly performed by an optician, who ensures the lenses are fitted into the customer’s frame of choice.

So now you know how glasses are made. If your curiosity still isn’t satisfied, look at some of our other informative articles in our Optical Centre, or ask our opticians if you don’t find the answers you’re looking for there.

The Comprehensive Guide to Sunglasses Lens Types

Have you ever stepped outside and the sun hits you like a spotlight, leaving you squinting and wishing you had better sunglasses?

We’ve all been there. But finding the right lenses isn’t just about avoiding that awkward sun squint—it’s about protecting your eyes and making sure you’re comfortable during any activity.

Whether you’re driving, hitting the trails, or just hanging out on a sunny day, the right pair of lenses can make all the difference.

In this guide, we’ll walk you through the different types of sunglasses lenses available to help you find the perfect pair for your lifestyle. We’ll break down the options to make sure your eyes are always protected, no matter where the day takes you.

Polarized Lenses

Polarized lenses are designed with a special coating to reduce glare from reflective surfaces like water, glass, and metal. By filtering out horizontal light waves, they provide sharper vision and reduce eye strain, especially in high-glare situations. An added bonus of Polarized lenses is they improve color contrast, which enhances your overall visual experience, particularly in outdoor settings.

Best For: Outdoor activities like fishing, driving, and water sports.

Product Example: Ray-Ban Polarized Sunglasses

Ray-Ban polarized sunglasses block over 99% of reflected light, providing superior protection against sun glare. Known for their durability and stylish frames, these lenses are scratch-resistant and offer 100% UV protection.

Mirrored Lenses

Mirrored lenses feature a reflective coating that limits the amount of light that enters your eyes. These lenses reduce glare while adding a sleek, stylish appearance. Their unique coating helps to reduce eye strain, especially in highly reflective conditions like snow or water.

Best For: High-glare environments and outdoor activities.

Product Example: Oakley Mirrored Sunglasses

Oakley mirrored sunglasses are designed for high-performance and style, featuring advanced technology that reduces glare while improving contrast and depth perception.

Gradient Lenses

Gradient lenses are tinted from top to bottom. The darkest part is at the top of the lens to shield your eyes from overhead sunlight while keeping the lower part lighter for better clarity. They are great for activities where light conditions change frequently, allowing for a comfortable transition between different lighting environments.

Best For: Driving and outdoor activities with varying light conditions.

Product Example: Maui Jim Gradient Sunglasses

Maui Jim gradient sunglasses offer superior comfort and protection. Their high-quality lenses provide 100% UV protection and are designed to reduce eye strain in changing light conditions.

Photochromic Lenses

Photochromic lenses adapt to changing light conditions. They darken when exposed to UV light and return to a lighter tint in low-light or indoor settings. These lenses provide a seamless transition between indoor and outdoor environments, eliminating the need to switch between regular glasses and sunglasses.

Best For: Situations where you are moving frequently between different lighting conditions.

Product Example: Transitions Signature Lenses

Transitions Signature lenses are renowned for their quick adaptability and superior clarity in various light conditions. These lenses maintain a neutral color balance, ensuring sharp vision throughout the day.

Prescription Sunglass Lenses

Prescription sunglass lenses combine vision correction with UV protection, giving you the convenience of having both in one pair. These lenses eliminate the need for clip-ons or switching between glasses and sunglasses, providing the same clarity as your regular prescription glasses but with the added benefit of sun protection.

Best For: Individuals who need vision correction and sun protection in one pair.

Product Example: Foster Grant Prescription Sunglasses

Foster Grant Prescription Sunglasses combine functionality and fashion, offering clear vision and full UV protection in one.
